Filth

It never out stays it’s welcome, challenges the viewer, makes your skin crawl, makes you laugh and gave me another reason to hate Michael Bay. What more could you want?

It’s visually uncertain and the editing is deliberately choppy but it is the choice of songs for the soundtrack that is the master stroke, it’s enjoyable and engaging without being over bearing. It is often at odds with the horrors being committed on screen. It all adds to this sense of unease and hyper-reality which makes the unsavoury elements of the story passable. Whilst I chastised Michael Bay’s festering scum pile of a film ‘Pain & Gain’ for relishing in the horrors we see the main characters commit. Here the viewer is well aware that the actions of the main character are whilst blackly funny, are not to be enjoyed.

McAvoy holds nothing back giving his performance the both barrels the role deserves. The brilliance is that he brings a humanity which perhaps wasn’t on the page. He takes a racist, homophobic, misogynistic , druggy, alcoholic , psychotic policeman and somehow finds a way to bring the viewer into his world without needing a sandpaper scrub. I loved Eddie Marsan as well though, the guy has incredible range. It is a career which has seen Marsan make me want to jump into a film and strangle his character or want to shake his hand and take him for a pint. Jim Broadbent’s Australian physiotherapist tapeworm (yep that’s what I said) is fantastically funny as well. Whilst the third act really goes over the edge into La La land and you may feel the attempts to redeem McAvoys character futile after the first half hour. As a film it is edgey and exciting and very well made. I never thought I’d be saying that about the guy that brought the world the Elijah Wood football hooligan movie.

Blue Jasmine

Strong direction on the part of Allen and a masterful performance by Blanchett make this worth separating yourself with some hard earn coin.

A lot of Woody Allen’s stuff leaves me wanting to go on a rampage in B&Q with it’s irritating dialogue and a quirky americanised view on life. But in this film he has found a lead whose performance matches his vision. Blanchett is horrid, self centred and unlikeable but the film manages to draw you to her as she descends throughout the film. The long closes ups of Blanchett display her ever eroding veil of reality. The flashbacks show the shallow image she used and a shield, the flash forwards showing it falling away. Even though she is surly, brash and pompous, underneath she broken and frail. Backed up by a excellent contrasting performance from Sally Hawkins as her sister who is all surface and has a down to earth attitude. It’s Woody Allen so there isn’t a lot of plot to pick at but the soundtrack is fraught and spikey much like our central character.

There is a horrendous CGI shot of a plane that opens the movie though. Seriously fire the team who created it.

Prisoners

Whilst it might be remember by me for the rudest audience I have ever dealt with, even by world of cine standards, nothing could distract from this taught intimate thriller. Whilst the final 3rd was a miss step I believe first hour and a half raises it to a level above your average studio piece.

Almost immediately there is a grim, tense atmosphere crafted by the film makers. The colour pallet is grey and bleak, the minimalist score leaves silences hanging in the frosty air of each scene. Character development is minimal, you certainly leave with unanswered questions about our leads, but I believe this was an attempt to allow viewers to place themselves in the film. I think is especially true when the violence escalates and the viewer is left to ask questions of their own morality. Performances are strong, Gyllenhaal’s driven but broken cop and Hugh Jacked-Mans over the edge father provide contrast but have rare screen time together.

I did enjoy those aspect but it is a shame it felt like it ran out of puff towards the end. Perhaps it had bitten off more weighty themes than it could chew, feeling hurried and poorly mapped towards a conclusion. In a two and half hour movie is some going.

N.B. A 16 bag McCoys multipack is not an appropriate snack in a cinema, Prisoners is not a date movie, silence in a film is not a cue for audience participation, phone calls from all but Henry Kissinger or the Dali Lama should be ignored!

About Time

No idea where to start with this, the very definition of a mixed bag. I will say that I went through the most ludicrous arch of hating everyone on screen to suddenly really identifying with the central character.

The film starts with a sequence which had me ready to bolt to the door immediately as we are introduced to classic Richard Curtis characters all quirky and middle class with seemingly no consideration for money or practicality. Moving out into the world our protagonist begins the disturbing, sugar coma romance which you think will be the heart of the film. I found it deeply worrying that the film never questions its own morality. The first hour is essentially the story a creepy loner, who discovers that he has an incredible ability then uses that ability to stalk and systematically manipulate a woman into falling in love with him. If this is your idea of a romantic comedy you really should take a step back and actually look at what’s going on screen its pretty bonkers. If you want to watch something in the same vain I recommend 2011’s Ruby Sparks, it does a far better job of it.

Suddenly once this trip into a Norman Bates fantasy land is complete the film takes a sharpe enough turn and manages to save itself from complete oblivion. It turns into a melodrama about the passage of time and Fathers and Sons. Whilst this isn’t perfect it’s handled extremely well. Curtis is nothing if not a master of manipulation hitting some universal recognisable emotional moments then twisting the knife with a heavily evocative soundtrack. It’s not subtle but it is effective.

There are plot holes the size of the Great Red spot on Jupiter and it just about manages to scrape a pass after the first hour but Curtis is too old to change his ways and he does this type of tosh (effective tosh) well.Oh a Bill Nighy is great, his absence during the ‘low grade foppish Hugh grant-alike meets impractically attractive American woman’ section is really felt.

Runner Runner

A very rare review posted immediately after a movie. Runner Runner is an irritating but short piece of fluff. It takes it’s self very seriously rarely breaking away to anything other than a mild titter. The problem being that it is a very generic plot that relies on its performers to sell it because you can see all the cringe worthy twists and turns you see coming a mile off. Unfortunately the central performance from Timberlake is very dull. It’s one note and he’s on screen 90% of the time. Gemma Arterton is thoroughly under used reduced to arm candy and being a minor plot device. Affleck is trying his best to be charming and menacing but comes off as just creepy. I wish Jack Nicholson from The Departed was in it to give you something interesting for people to play off.

My major issue though was with the message, Timberlakes character is an arrogant scum bag who displays the kind of hubris that deserves prison. I really wanted Affleck to feed him to a crocodile after 5 mins, but is played sympathetically. SPOILERS he gets away with the girl and a shed load of stolen money and the FBI seems ok with this!

It’s not as gritty, smart or slick as he thinks it is. Watch on a plane if you have to.

R.I.P.D

The central conceit with this film was that even a ropey comic book property could be converted into box office gold. The Hollywood machine did its number crunching and figured that if you get stars + comic books+ action = big sheds of money.

What’s left is a film which feels tired, lazy and gobsmackingly unoriginal. Stealing the plot of Ghost and putting it in a Men in Black-lite universe. The performances are lazy the usually solid Jeff Bridges tries desperately to ham it up but his Rooster Cockburn meets Yosemite Sam is a one beat drum which gets old very quickly. Ryan Reynolds phones it in as he has to act next to some second rate CGI work again, for a film he is supposed to be dead in he certainly looked dead behind the eyes. Mary Louise Parker is mentally counting her pay check to this and Red 2 in every scene. Kevin Bacon is Kevin Bacon.

Special mention to the CGI it was really terrible noticeably so and when the film is so tedious I tend to look at even more. It’s pretty unforgivable in this day and age especially when you are throwing hundreds of millions of dollars away on shite like this.

In A World…

I really wanted to like this but it landed with a bit of a damp squib. Perhaps if Lake Bell got someone to help her out it might have tightened up some of the sloppier elements of the story and generally awkward elements of the filmmaking. It is an admirable effort by Bell who wrote, directed, produced and starred in the film. It’s an interesting concept and seems rife for picking apart for comedy. Unfortunately there is an uneven nature to the film which, whilst potentially was intentional, really grated on me. Sub-plots meander in and out of the central premise and build towards an incredibly convoluted conclusion

It’s hard not to compare this with Frances Ha who managed to execute a similar premise with greater style and a subtlety. Demitri Martin does an excellent turn as the love interest, I’ve never been a big fan but this is by far the best thing I’ve seen him in. As he becomes more involved as the film moves towards the third act it felt like it was gathering pace and I started to really enjoy it. But that pace suddenly drops out. An uneven disappointment.

Pain & Gain

I’ve been putting off this review it’s been like putting off going to the dentist. Just by thinking about it too much is like have a root canal.

It goes without saying that even when trying to do a movie which is different to his usual knuckle dragging CGI snooze fests Bay maintains the humour of an internet troll. Any chance to get a cheap laugh out of weight, gender, colour or ethnicity are leapt upon (as long as it’s not his perceived audience of teenage American white male of medium build). Our central characters are devoid of merit and difficult to like, which isn’t uncommon ground for a black comedy. But this is ‘based on a true story’ it’s on the posters/trailers he even tells us this twice, once mid film. In making that distinction Bay should show the horror of the brutal and disturbing nature of that story. Instead it’s cheap laughs and a relish for the black heart of the film. Bay wants you to enjoy the disgust and worryingly he seems to relish it too. In doing so he reveals more of himself than of the characters.

Avoid.

23/09/13

Rush

‘A film based on true story’ it’s such an absurd thing to say. I guess ‘a cinematic presentation of events which may or may not of happened, using an interpretation of facts we have available, whilst manipulating them to increase dramatic tension’ wouldn’t look as good on a poster. It must be said though that Ron Howard and Peter Morgan do have a strong track record in bringing dramatic recreations of historical figures and events to the screen (Apollo 13, A Beautiful Mind, Frost/Nixon, The Queen, Last King of Scotland). Whilst the facts are twisted for dramatic effect the films themselves are usually about telling you something more than a recreation of events. If you listen to Mark Kermode enough you know the adage ‘Jaws isn’t about a shark’.


The reason for such a daft intro is that I really wanted Rush to be about something more than one character is reckless and one is disciplined. These traits are established but never fully explored. Hemsworth lives it up womanising, drinking and donning his British accent from the Thor movies, which was quite distracting. But unlike his character rarely has to push himself. Daniel Bruhls performance is far more complex and interesting. However he did have the larger chunk of dramatic scenes due to the real life events. That said what elevates this film from the frustratingly one dimensional are the race sequences themselves. They are far more visceral and engaging than a blockbuster action sequence. The drama, danger, speed and noise are captured without feeling the need to have our two main characters locking wheel to wheel in head to head races. It will be porn for F1 fans and petrol heads, though I found it excellent and I don’t count myself as either. Difficulty is it takes a while to get to a lot of this and in-between the character development is limited. The other success’s are the visuals, whilst there are a few dodgy green screen shots, it doesn’t few like cgi overload. The tone and feel of the era are well captured.


I did enjoy the film but felt myself wanting more, given the track record of those involved and how much I enjoyed the scenes on the track. The off track stuff feels like a damp squib after you’ve seen it.
